Risk by day of week

What this shows: for each day of the week, the share of crashes that led to someone being hurt. The dashed line is the overall average across the whole dataset. How we calculate it: we count crashes with injuries and divide by all crashes for that day. For very small groups, numbers can bounce around, so we gently pull those tiny groups toward the overall average. Think of it as a small stabilizer that keeps outliers from being misleading. How to read it: bars above the dashed line suggest a riskier‑than‑average day; bars below suggest a calmer day, all else equal.

Risk by vehicle type

What this shows: among the most common vehicle types, the share of crashes that led to injuries. The dashed line marks the overall average. How we calculate it: for each vehicle type we divide injury crashes by all crashes of that type. Rare categories can swing wildly, so we focus on the common types and lightly steady the estimates so tiny groups don’t mislead. How to read it: bars to the right of the dashed line suggest a higher‑than‑average chance of injury for that vehicle type in this dataset; bars to the left suggest lower.

Male vs female drivers

What this shows: for each recorded driver sex (Male, Female, and sometimes Other/Unknown), the share of crashes that involved an injury. The dashed line shows the overall average. How we calculate it: we take injury crashes divided by all crashes for each group. To keep tiny groups from swinging wildly, we apply a light stabilizer that nudges very small samples toward the overall average. How to read it: taller bars mean a higher‑than‑average chance that a crash involved injuries for that group in this dataset; shorter bars mean lower.

Chapter 1 · Place

See where risk lives

How this map is made: we read the crash file, pick rows that have a valid date, time, and location, and turn each crash into a dot on the map. The dots stay the same visual size as you zoom, so you can still see clusters when zoomed out. The Hour slider simply shows crashes that happened in that hour (for example, 7:00–7:59). What to look for: clusters reveal busy, risk‑heavy streets and junctions; commute hours spread activity along major corridors; late‑night hours concentrate in fewer spots.

Chapter 2 · Time

Find calmer hours

How this heatmap is made: we make a simple grid of time—months across the top and hours down the side—and count how many crashes fall into each square. Darker, warmer colors mean “more here” relative to what you’re looking at. You can zoom the time axis or reorder the hours to compare patterns. About the color and toggle: by default (Global), colors compare all squares to each other, so very busy months look hotter overall. Switch to Per column to compare hours within each month (each column is scaled to itself); this is great for seeing daily patterns even in quieter months. Either way, you’re seeing “where is it higher vs lower,” not exact unit totals.

Chapter 3 · Why

Choose better defaults

What this shows: which conditions tend to go with injuries more often than we’d expect by chance. Examples include driver actions, vehicle types, hours, and contributing factors. How we rank them: for each condition we compare its injury share with the overall average and give it a simple “are these connected?” score (a standard statistics check). Items rise to the top when the difference is real and based on many crashes. Color shows direction: red means higher than average; green means lower. Tip: focus on the top bars with large counts.

Estimate injury risk

Enter a few basics about the situation. The estimator uses a regularized statistical model trained on this dataset to predict the chance that a crash involved injuries for that combination. It guards against overfitting by sharing information across similar groups.

Method: L2‑regularized logistic regression on hashed features (vehicle type, action, borough, hour, day of week), trained on this file’s records. Estimates are probabilities in this dataset, not guarantees.

Best vs worst risk chains

What this shows: two chains built from the five estimator inputs (vehicle type, vehicle action, borough, hour, day of week). Starting from an empty scenario, we add one choice at a time — always picking the option that most increases the model’s predicted injury risk (worst chain) or most decreases it (best chain) while staying grounded in enough real records. How to read it: each step shows the chosen condition, the estimated injury probability for the chain up to that point, and how many crashes in the data match that combination. Think of it as “if you also add this, risk goes up/down this much.”
